WRITING POETRY

with Sachi






I have been told for much of my life to be scared
of the hours past eight;
to walk in a curve around alleys,
and stand so that no one can push me into the train tracks.

I am envious of the feeling those boys have,
their ability to pull over the car in Idaho and step out without shoes,
to jump on the beach and climb over the rocks.

They meander through the empty streets and shores—
I walk.
My voice goes mute,
my body cold.

But there are no ticks on the beach
and no one to hurt you in Stonington.
I’ve never really been one for poetry. I tend to sit out poetry readings when I can, and dreaded the inevitable poetry unit in my high school classes. I’ve always felt a bit of hypocrisy, though, given my interest in music and appreciation for good song lyrics. When I write passages of words in my journal, I call it prose, but never poetry. But really, what’s the difference? In the name of exposure therapy, I had my friend Sachi, a writer of poems, walk me through their writing process.
"I just start off with random words or lines I hear that sound good,” they tell me, “then I can just build off that one line and think of the theme of the poem from there.” After a bit of prodding, they list off a couple words they have sitting in their notes app, waiting for when the time is right. Included are “fish monger”, “almanac”, “flute”, and “beak”. I’ve never really thought about collecting words like this, holding them as inspiration simply because of the way they sound or the feelings they elicit. I note in my head to start doing this.
Sachi says they like to write about the ocean. They like its color and its vastness- its poetic nature. They say they only got into poetry about a year and a half ago after writing a poem about someone who they’re now dating, someone I know from Chicago. I read Sachi’s poem- it’s interesting to hear about the ways that they think of love and their partner. I was moved. We talk about the structure of the poem and how they used times of day to create a cohesive piece. After telling them what I want to write about, they suggest ways I could similarly structure my writing.
We talk about rhythm in poetry and how it might be used as a differentiating point from prose. Sachi says they just feel out the rhythm when they write, not following any specific rules. “I don’t really do the right stuff with gramatics either,” they say while laughing, “with poetry no one can tell you it's wrong”. The boundlessness of poetry has always been appealing to me- in whatever form it is written, it can’t really be disputable. Rules don’t really exist, maybe that’s why I’ve had a hard time getting into it. As someone who appreciates guidelines, it’s intimidating to start on something so wide open.
Sachi asks to see what I’ve written so far- I sheepishly laugh and refuse to show them, growing redder and redder. Finally, in the spirit of this project, I force myself to show them. I realize it's not so bad- what I’ve written and the feeling of showing Sachi what I wrote. We go through the poem and they note where I’m being repetitive and where I need more detail. They keep apologizing for not really knowing what to tell me about writing poetry. “I just kind of do it,” they tell me. I tell them that not only have they already been infinitely helpful, but that this is the point of the whole thing. To teach each other what we do know, even if it's not all of it.
